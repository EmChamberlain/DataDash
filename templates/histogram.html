Understanding a Histogram:

Summary: A histogram provides a visual interpretation of the number of data points that lie within a range of values. These ranges of values are called classes or bins. The frequency of the data that falls in each class is depicted by the use of a bar. Histograms are useful for viewing how the data is distributed.

Shapes: A histogram can take on several different types of distributions, as depicted below:

Symmetrical: This is otherwise known as a normal distribution. Scores are equally likely to occur on either side of the average. This means that the average is an overall accurate depiction of the center value.

Left Skew: A left skew would mean the distribution is skewed left, towards the higher values. This means that there were few people that did extrordinarily well on the test, and a majority only did okay or poorly.

Right Skew: A right skew would mean the distribution is skewed right, towards the lower values. This means that there were many people that did okay and very well, and there were a few people who did exceptionally poor.

Uniform: A uniform histogram is hard to gain information from. It mostly means that the class did evenly well across the the different score ranges chosen for the histogram.

Bimodal/Multimodal: A multimodal shape has two or more peaks. This means that the test scores peaked in two different areas, some centered around a higher score, other centered around a lower score.
